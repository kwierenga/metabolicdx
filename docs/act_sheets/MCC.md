# ACT sheet — 3-Methylcrotonyl-CoA carboxylase (3-MCC) deficiency (MCC)

**Urgency:** ROUTINE

Routine within 2–4 weeks. Most NBS-identified 3-MCC is clinically benign — but the C5-OH pattern can also signal treatable MCD/biotinidase deficiency or MATERNAL 3-MCC, both of which need separate management.

## Confirmatory testing

1. **Repeat plasma acylcarnitines** — confirm C5-OH elevation.
2. **Urine organic acids** — 3-methylcrotonylglycine (MCG) + 3-OH-isovaleric acid.
3. **Plasma biotinidase activity — RULE OUT biotinidase / MCD first** (treatable, biotin-responsive).
4. **Plasma free + total carnitine** — often low (secondary).
5. **MATERNAL biotinidase activity AND maternal urine MCG** — NBS detection is sometimes due to maternal MCC or biotinidase deficiency, not infant disease.
6. **MCCC1 / MCCC2 molecular analysis** — confirms infant disease.

## Pitfalls and false-positive causes

1. **MATERNAL 3-MCC deficiency** — placental / breast-milk transfer produces a positive infant NBS that resolves on formula feeding; the most common cause of positive C5-OH NBS.
2. **Maternal biotinidase deficiency** — same picture.
3. **MCD / biotinidase / HLCS deficiency in INFANT** — must be excluded first; treatable with biotin.
4. **HMG-CoA lyase deficiency** — also high C5-OH; distinguished by hypoketotic hypoglycaemia and HMG in urine.
5. **β-Ketothiolase, isolated MCD** — share C5-OH elevation.
6. **Hydroxylated AC of generic mitochondrial dysfunction** — non-specific elevation.

# ACT sheet — Classic galactosaemia (GALT deficiency) (GALT)

**Urgency:** CRITICAL

Same-day callback. Acute liver failure and E. coli sepsis present in the first week if galactose continues; remove ALL galactose/lactose immediately on suspicion (well before confirmatory results return).

## Confirmatory testing

1. **Erythrocyte GALT enzyme activity** — definitive for classic galactosemia; MUST be performed before any blood transfusion (or molecular substituted).
2. **Erythrocyte galactose-1-phosphate (Gal-1-P)** — markedly elevated; monitors dietary control thereafter.
3. **GALT molecular analysis** — c.563A>G (p.Q188R) most common classic; Duarte variant (p.N314D) milder. Essential when transfusion makes enzyme assay unreliable.
4. **Plasma galactose, urine reducing substances, Beutler test (DBS)** — supportive markers.
5. **Liver function, coagulation, sepsis workup** — if any clinical concern; E. coli sepsis is the classical lethal complication.
6. **Ophthalmology** — cataracts can develop within weeks of continued galactose exposure.

## Pitfalls and false-positive causes

1. **Recent blood transfusion (within 90 days)** — donor RBC GALT activity gives false-NORMAL result; cannot rule out classic galactosemia by enzyme assay until 90 days post-transfusion. Use molecular instead.
2. **Duarte variant (D/G compound heterozygote)** — partial deficiency (~25% activity); usually clinically benign and most centres no longer impose lifelong restriction.
3. **Heterozygote GALT carriers** — ~50% activity; clinically silent.
4. **Galactokinase (GALK1) deficiency** — galactose elevated but Gal-1-P NORMAL; isolated cataracts only.
5. **Galactose epimerase (GALE) deficiency, generalised form** — elevated Gal-1-P but normal GALT activity.
6. **Liver disease of any cause** — can secondarily impair GALT activity on assay.
7. **Heat-degraded DBS** — falsely-low activity on Beutler test; repeat with fresh sample.

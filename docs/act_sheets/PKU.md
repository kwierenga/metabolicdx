# ACT sheet — Phenylketonuria (PKU) / hyperphenylalaninaemia (PKU)

**Urgency:** ROUTINE

Routine within 1–2 weeks. Untreated PKU does not produce harm in the first months — early diagnosis allows preventive dietary intervention before brain development is impaired.

## Confirmatory testing

1. **Repeat plasma amino acids** — quantify Phe, Tyr and the Phe/Tyr ratio (>3 suspicious; >10 classical PKU).
2. **Urine pterins (neopterin + biopterin) AND DBS DHPR activity** — MUST be performed in every newborn with hyperphenylalaninaemia to exclude BH4 disorders (1–3% of HPA, different therapy).
3. **BH4 loading test (20 mg/kg)** — ≥30% Phe reduction at 24 h identifies BH4-responsive PAH variants (sapropterin candidates).
4. **PAH molecular analysis** — predicts BH4-responsiveness (e.g., p.Y414C, p.A403V responsive; p.R408W classic non-responsive).
5. **CSF neurotransmitter metabolites (HVA, 5-HIAA, 5-MTHF)** — only if pterins suggest BH4 disorder.

## Pitfalls and false-positive causes

1. **TPN / amino acid infusion** — protein loading transiently elevates Phe; repeat off TPN.
2. **Premature infants** — immature PAH activity gives mild HPA that resolves.
3. **Tyrosinaemia (TYR1/2/3)** — Phe rises secondary to liver dysfunction with markedly higher Tyr; succinylacetone identifies TYR1.
4. **Liver dysfunction of any cause** — secondary HPA, with Tyr and Met also elevated.
5. **Maternal PKU syndrome** — high NBS Phe in well infant born to a poorly-controlled PKU mother; not infant disease.
6. **Missing a BH4 disorder** — same Phe profile as PAH deficiency, but diet alone causes neurological deterioration; this is the most catastrophic interpretive error in PKU workup.
